# Schwab's Record Quarter Quietly Bet Its Margin on the Fed Never Cutting Again

*The Charles Schwab Corporation just printed the cleanest quarter of its post-pandemic life — net revenue up 16% to $6.48 billion, GAAP profit up 30% to $2.48 billion, net interest margin clawing back to 2.88% from 2.53% a year earlier, and the dreaded high-cost supplemental funding that nearly broke the equity story in 2023 paid down 77% to roughly $5.1 billion. Management is now tracking above its own $5.70–$5.80 full-year EPS scenario. But read the fine print and a different machine emerges: the margin recovery management is celebrating is now explicitly underwritten by a forward curve that prices in no rate cuts in 2026 — Schwab's own CFO said the firm benefits from a "lack of rate cuts." A company that nearly drowned when rates went up in 2023 is now quietly priced for rates to stay up forever. The bond book is still underwater. The capital cushion is at its self-imposed floor. And the stock, having round-tripped the crisis, fell on the record print. This is what cyclical normalization priced as secular destiny looks like.*

There is a particular kind of corporate comeback that should make a careful investor more nervous, not less. It is the comeback where every wound has healed, every metric has reverted, every analyst has moved on — and the company, flush with relief, quietly rebuilds its entire profit engine on the precise macro assumption that nearly killed it eighteen months earlier. The Charles Schwab Corporation delivered exactly that quarter in April 2026, and almost nobody noticed the sleight of hand. The headline numbers were genuinely strong. The structure underneath them is a bet, dressed as a recovery.

Schwab reported first-quarter 2026 net revenue of $6.48 billion, up 16% year-over-year. GAAP net income reached $2.48 billion, a 30% increase, with diluted earnings per share of $1.37 against $0.99 a year earlier. On an adjusted basis the firm earned $1.43, edging consensus of roughly $1.42 by a single penny. Net interest margin — the spread that is the beating heart of any bank-inside-a-brokerage — expanded to 2.88% from 2.53% in the prior-year quarter. Total client assets hit $11.77 trillion, up 19%. Core net new assets came in at $140.0 billion. Daily average trades reached a record 9.9 million, up 34%. By any reasonable accounting, this was the best quarter Schwab has printed since the 2023 deposit panic. And yet the stock fell. The disconnect is the story.

## The crisis the bulls have decided is over

To understand why a record quarter is a warning, you have to remember what broke in 2023. Schwab's defining vulnerability was never its brokerage — it was the bank bolted onto it. When the Federal Reserve raised rates at the fastest pace in four decades, Schwab's clients did what economically rational people do: they "sorted" their idle cash out of Schwab's near-zero-yield sweep deposits and into money-market funds and Treasury bills paying 5%. Deposits drained. To fund the gap without crystallizing losses on a long-duration bond book that had cratered in value, Schwab leaned on the most expensive money available — Federal Home Loan Bank advances and brokered certificates of deposit. At the peak, that supplemental funding ballooned to tens of billions of dollars, and the market correctly read it as a tax on every future dollar of net interest income. The stock lost more than a third of its value.

Now look at the 2026 scorecard. Bank deposits stood at $253.0 billion, up 3% year-over-year — growing again, not shrinking. Supplemental funding has been crushed: management states it has reduced bank supplemental funding by roughly $50.0 billion, or 77%, since September 30, 2024, ending the first quarter at approximately $5.1 billion. The expensive money is nearly gone. Net interest revenue rose 16% to $3.1 billion precisely because those higher-cost borrowings are being retired and lower-yielding legacy assets are maturing into a higher-rate world. On the surface, the wound has closed cleanly.

The bull case treats this as a permanent return to a structural earnings power that was merely interrupted. The forensic reading is colder: this is a textbook cyclical normalization, and the market is now capitalizing the recovered run-rate as if it were the new floor rather than a cyclical peak. The difference between those two interpretations is, quite literally, the entire valuation.

## The margin is now long the Fed doing nothing

Here is the line that should have led every story and led none of them. Schwab entered 2026 guiding to a $5.70–$5.80 full-year EPS scenario that assumed two Federal Reserve rate cuts. By the time it reported the first quarter, the forward curve had shifted toward no cuts in 2026 — and management's reaction was not caution but enthusiasm. Chief Financial Officer Michael Verdeschi framed the absence of rate cuts as a tailwind, telling the market that net interest margin "could benefit from a lack of rate cuts" and that there was "upward momentum" expected for both NIM and client cash growth. The firm now describes itself as tracking above that original EPS range.

Sit with the irony. In 2023, rising rates were the catastrophe — they triggered the cash sorting, gutted the bond book, and forced the high-cost funding. In 2026, the company's earnings upside is explicitly contingent on rates staying elevated. The very macro regime that nearly broke Schwab is now the one its profit forecast is leaning on. That is not a hedge. That is the same trade with the sign flipped, and the market is paying full price for the optimistic side of it.

The asymmetry is brutal once you map it. If the Fed holds, Schwab's NIM grinds higher and the bulls are vindicated — modestly, because much of that is already in estimates. But if the Fed cuts, two things happen at once that the celebratory narrative glosses over. First, the reinvestment math that is currently lifting NIM — old low-yield assets maturing into higher yields — slows or reverses as new money gets put to work at lower rates. Second, and more dangerously, lower short rates re-narrow the gap between Schwab's sweep deposits and competing cash products only if Schwab passes the cut through; if it doesn't, the cash-sorting impulse that defined 2023 can return. A company priced for the good outcome of a binary macro bet, where the bad outcome reopens its original wound, is not a low-risk normalization story. It is a leveraged rate position wearing a brokerage logo.

## The bond book never actually healed — it just stopped being shown to you

The cleanest tell in the entire filing is what Schwab is *not* required to mark. The improvement in average accumulated other comprehensive income that management cites approvingly came from "lower unrealized losses" on available-for-sale securities and from the amortization of losses on securities previously transferred from AFS to held-to-maturity. Read that again: the losses are being *amortized*, which is the accounting verb for a loss that was real, was reclassified, and is now being dribbled back into earnings over time rather than recognized at once. In the first quarter alone, amortization of amounts previously recorded upon transfer to HTM was $496 million.

This is the magic trick at the center of every regional-bank rate story since Silicon Valley Bank, and Schwab runs a sophisticated version of it. When a bond portfolio is held available-for-sale, its mark-to-market losses flow through AOCI and dent reported book value. When those same bonds are reclassified to held-to-maturity, they are carried at amortized cost and the paper loss stops bouncing equity around — even though the economic loss is identical until the bonds either mature or are sold. Schwab moved a large slug of duration into HTM during the crisis precisely to stop the bleeding from appearing. The underwater position did not disappear; it was relocated to a part of the balance sheet investors are encouraged not to look at.

There is a further layer. Schwab has elected to opt out of including most components of AOCI in its regulatory capital calculations — a permitted choice that means even the AFS losses that *do* hit book equity are excluded from the ratios regulators and the market use to judge capital adequacy. So the unrealized loss is (a) partly hidden in HTM at amortized cost, and (b) where it isn't hidden, explicitly excluded from the capital optics. None of this is fraud; all of it is disclosed; every regional bank with a long bond book does some version of it. But an honest forensic accounting of Schwab's "recovery" has to state plainly that a meaningful chunk of the bond-book damage from 2023 has not been repaired — it has been administratively de-emphasized, and is healing only at the glacial pace that bonds pull to par. The book value the market is paying a premium to is flattered by accounting elections, not just by operations.

## A capital cushion sitting on its own floor

Quality-of-earnings work is incomplete without quality-of-capital work, and here Schwab gives you something concrete to worry about. The consolidated Tier 1 leverage ratio was 8.9% at March 31, 2026 — down from 9.3% at year-end 2025. The consolidated *adjusted* Tier 1 leverage ratio, which is the metric Schwab itself manages to, sat at 6.8%, down from 7.1% at year-end, and squarely inside the firm's stated long-term operating objective of 6.75% to 7.00%.

Notice what "inside the operating objective" actually means when you are at 6.8% against a 6.75% floor: there is almost no daylight. Schwab returned $2.4 billion to shareholders in buybacks during the quarter and raised the dividend 19% — generous capital return that is itself part of why the leverage ratio drifted down. A company simultaneously (a) carrying an unamortized bond-book loss it has elected to keep out of capital, (b) running its managed capital ratio at the very bottom of its target band, and (c) accelerating buybacks into that position is making an implicit bet that nothing forces it to recognize those bond losses or rebuild capital quickly. That bet is fine in a placid market. It is exactly the kind of thin margin that turns a manageable shock into a forced one — and 2023 is the proof of concept for how fast Schwab's specific balance sheet can go from comfortable to constrained.

## The denominator that is doing the heavy lifting

Schwab's favorite number, repeated in every release, is total client assets: $11.77 trillion, up 19%. It is a genuinely impressive figure and an effective piece of misdirection. The vast majority of that $11.77 trillion is client securities Schwab merely custodies — assets it does not own, does not fund, and earns only thin custody and advice fees on. The number that actually drives the bank's profit engine is far smaller and growing far slower: bank deposits of $253.0 billion, up just 3%. The 19% headline is mostly market appreciation and asset gathering at the brokerage; it tells you very little about the health of the high-margin balance-sheet business that is the entire reason Schwab trades like a bank rather than a pure asset-gatherer.

This is the denominator illusion in its native habitat. When the market rallies, client assets balloon and Schwab looks like it is compounding at a spectacular clip; the per-asset economics can be deteriorating underneath and the headline still goes up and to the right. Strip out market beta and ask the harder question — how fast is the *funded, spread-earning* book actually growing organically? At 3% deposit growth, the answer is: slowly, and in a way that is hostage to whether clients keep their cash parked in low-yield sweeps rather than sweeping it into Schwab's own higher-yielding money funds and purchased CDs. Every dollar that migrates from a cheap sweep deposit to a client-directed cash product is a dollar that stops being a high-margin funding source and becomes, at best, a low-fee one. The 19% number is real. It is also not the number that determines whether the stock is cheap.

## Trading revenue is the most cyclical dollar in the building

The other star of the quarter was trading: 9.9 million daily average trades, up 34%, driving trading revenue up roughly 20%. Bulls fold this into the secular growth story. It belongs in the cyclical column, and it belongs there emphatically. Daily average trades are a near-perfect proxy for retail risk appetite, and risk appetite in early 2026 was elevated — the same froth visible across meme equities, options volumes, and speculative flows. A 34% jump in trade count is not a structural improvement in Schwab's franchise; it is a reading on the temperature of the retail investor, and that temperature mean-reverts violently. When the next drawdown arrives, daily average trades do not gently normalize — they fall off a cliff, and so does the revenue line that just grew 20%.

Capitalizing peak trading activity at a normalized multiple is one of the oldest valuation traps in financial services. Schwab has more of these procyclical revenue lines stacked on top of each other than almost any peer: trading volumes, asset-management fees that scale with market levels, and a NIM that is currently riding the high end of the rate cycle. Each one is independently near a cyclical high. The probability that all three stay at these levels simultaneously is the probability the bull case is implicitly assigning — and it is lower than the price implies.

## Priced for the recovery to be the destination

Pull it together and the valuation question sharpens. SCHW changed hands around the low $90s in June 2026, with a trailing P/E in the high-teens — roughly 17.65 on one mid-June reading — and a forward P/E near 15x on the elevated 2026 estimate. On its face, 15x forward looks undemanding against an S&P 500 that has averaged closer to 19x. But a forward multiple is only as cheap as its denominator is durable, and Schwab's forward earnings denominator is built from a stack of cyclical highs: peak-cycle NIM that needs rates to stay up, peak retail trading that needs froth to persist, and asset-based fees that need the market not to fall. Apply a single recessionary or rate-cutting shock and that "cheap" 15x is suddenly 15x a number that no longer exists.

That is why the stock fell on a record quarter. The market, in its wiser moments, understands that you do not pay up for earnings that are good *because* the cycle is good. The bull is buying normalized earnings power. The bear is pointing out that "normalized" and "peak" have become the same number, and that the company itself has told you its upside requires the Fed to do nothing — which is to say, requires the macro regime that previously detonated its balance sheet to politely persist.

## What the bulls genuinely get right

It would be dishonest to leave the impression that Schwab is a broken company papering over a hole. It is not, and the bull case has real teeth that deserve a fair hearing. Start with the deposit recovery, which is the single most important fact and which the bears underweighted for two years: deposits are growing again, supplemental funding has been slashed 77% to roughly $5.1 billion, and the high-cost-funding death spiral that the most aggressive 2023 shorts predicted simply did not happen. Schwab funded its way through the worst rate shock in forty years without a capital raise, without a dividend cut on the common, and without a forced bond sale at the lows. That is genuine resilience, and pretending otherwise would be exactly the kind of motivated analysis this piece is supposed to avoid.

The franchise itself is also formidable in ways that are not cyclical at all. $11.77 trillion in client assets and 1.3 million new brokerage accounts in a single quarter reflect a brand and a distribution machine that genuinely compound. The TD Ameritrade integration — the source of years of attrition fear — is now substantially behind the company, and core net new assets of $140.0 billion suggest the dreaded client defection never materialized at scale. The scale advantage is real: Schwab can offer pricing and breadth few competitors can match, and asset-gathering at this magnitude is a durable, low-capital annuity that throws off fees regardless of the rate cycle. The 19% dividend increase and $2.4 billion of buybacks, whatever they imply about the capital cushion, also reflect a board that sees years of free cash flow ahead and is not wrong to. And on the rate bet specifically, the bulls have history on their side in one respect: if rates do stay higher for longer — a scenario plenty of serious people assign meaningful probability — Schwab's NIM expansion has real room to run, and the earnings could grind above even the raised guidance. The thesis here is not that Schwab is bad. It is that Schwab is good, cyclical, and priced as though it were good and secular — and that the gap between those two words is where the risk lives.

## The kicker

Every great financial-services blow-up rhymes the same way: a fundamentally sound franchise, a balance sheet quietly optimized for one macro regime, and a market willing to capitalize the best stretch of the cycle as the new permanent baseline. Schwab is not insolvent, not fraudulent, and not even badly run — its deposit recovery is real and its franchise is a fortress. But the firm has rebuilt its entire margin story on the assumption that the Federal Reserve never cuts again, kept the unhealed half of its 2023 bond losses in the amortized-cost shadows and out of regulatory capital, run its managed leverage ratio down to the very floor of its own target band while accelerating buybacks, and stacked three independently cyclical revenue lines — peak NIM, peak trading, market-linked fees — into a single forward number the Street now calls "cheap." The stock fell on a record quarter because some part of the market already knows what the celebratory headlines do not say out loud.

*The bond book did not heal; it was reclassified, and the recovery everyone is buying is a bet that the same rates which nearly drowned Schwab in 2023 will stay high enough, and steady enough, to keep the rescue from ever being tested again.*
