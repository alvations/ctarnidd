# Cashing Out the Machine

*The founders built the most talked-about company of the AI boom, sold the public a 150% rally, and quietly converted $2.3 billion of their own paper into cash. Not one of them has bought a single share back.*

**Related tickers:** `CRWV` (CoreWeave) · `NVDA` (Nvidia — backer, supplier and backstop customer) · `MSFT` (Microsoft — anchor customer) · `META` (Meta) · `BTC` (bitcoin — the business it used to be).

---

Every quarter, in the routine exhaust of regulatory filings that nobody is meant to read closely, a small machine prints the same message in slightly different numbers. A Form 4 here. An automated 10b5-1 disposal there. A few hundred thousand shares, a few tens of millions of dollars, a name at the top that belongs to one of the three men who built CoreWeave out of a failed crypto-mining outfit and rode it to one of the most spectacular debuts of the artificial-intelligence era.

Read one of those filings and it is nothing. Read all of them together — the way the insider-tracking firm Washington Service does, the way a forensic accountant would — and a different picture resolves. Since CoreWeave's stock came unlocked, the company's co-founders have sold more than **$2.3 billion** of it. Over the same stretch, across hundreds of insider transactions, the number of shares any insider has *bought* on the open market is, as best the filings show, zero.

That is the whole story in one line, and it is worth sitting with before the noise of the bull case drowns it out. The people with the most complete view of this business — the men who know exactly what its GPUs are worth, what its contracts really say, and how fast its hardware is melting — have spent the past ten months turning their conviction into cash. The people being asked to buy what they are selling are told to focus on the growth.

## The tell

In a poker game, the move that matters is rarely the one a player announces. It is the involuntary one — the tell. CoreWeave's tell is the tape of its own insiders.

Chief among the sellers is co-founder Brian Venturo, who has personally offloaded more than **$1.1 billion** in stock, enough to rank him among the very largest individual insider sellers anywhere on the US market this year. Alongside him, chief executive Michael Intrator and co-founder Brannin McBee have steadily reduced their own stakes. Together the three have cut their combined holdings by roughly a quarter and now control something in the neighborhood of **18%** of the company — down from the founder-dominant cap table they carried into the IPO. In one recent sixty-day window the filings logged dozens of insider sales; across the past year, something on the order of two hundred. The buy column stayed empty the entire time.

The standard defense is ready to hand, and CoreWeave will offer it: these are **10b5-1 plans**, pre-arranged sales scheduled in advance precisely so that executives cannot be accused of trading on inside information. That is true, and it is not as exculpatory as it sounds. A 10b5-1 plan tells you *when* a sale executes; it does not tell you *why* the executive chose to schedule a sale of that size at all. A founder who believes the next five years will be better than the last does not, as a rule, arrange to systematically dismantle his position the moment the lockup lifts. The plan launders the timing. It does not launder the decision.

And the founders are not alone at the exits. Magnetar Financial, the hedge fund whose early backing helped turn CoreWeave from a curiosity into a contender, has sold something like **$5.5 billion** in stock, cutting its stake to under 10%. Nvidia, which holds roughly 6%, is the rare large holder *not* selling — for reasons that, as we will see, have less to do with faith than with the fact that Nvidia is selling CoreWeave something far more valuable than shares.

## The melting machine

To understand why the people inside might be in a hurry, you have to understand what CoreWeave actually owns. Strip away the language of "AI cloud" and "hyperscale infrastructure" and the company is, at bottom, a landlord renting out a warehouse full of graphics chips. Its entire business is buying enormous quantities of Nvidia GPUs on borrowed money and renting time on them to companies training AI models.

The problem with that business is the central, awkward fact of the hardware: it does not age like a building. It ages like fruit.

CoreWeave depreciates its GPUs over a useful life of roughly six years — a figure it quietly raised from five back in 2023, a change that, conveniently, lowers the depreciation expense booked against each quarter's revenue and flatters the bottom line. Many people who study this hardware for a living think six years is fantasy. Data-center GPUs run flat out at high utilization; credible estimates of their economically useful life run as short as **one to three years** before a newer, faster, more power-efficient generation makes the old silicon uncompetitive. Michael Burry — the investor who shorted the housing bubble — has gone public with the accusation that the big AI infrastructure players are systematically understating depreciation by stretching useful lives, and has put the cumulative gap across the largest five at something like **$176 billion** between 2026 and 2028.

The numbers inside CoreWeave's own filings show why this is not an abstraction. In a single recent quarter the company booked **$1.14 billion** of depreciation — more than double the figure a year earlier — against revenue of roughly **$2.07 billion**. More than half of every dollar of revenue, in other words, is already being consumed by the accounting recognition that the machines are wearing out. And that is at the *generous* six-year assumption. Shorten the useful life toward what the skeptics believe is real, and the depreciation line swallows the business whole.

CoreWeave's answer is that its older chips are holding their value better than the bears claim — that 2020-era A100s remain fully booked and 2022-era H100s still command most of their original rental prices. Perhaps. But that is an argument about the recent past, made at the top of a capacity shortage, by people who are simultaneously selling their stock as fast as the lockups allow. The tell, again, undercuts the testimony.

## The debt under the ice

A melting asset would be survivable if it were paid for. CoreWeave's is not.

The company has built its warehouse of depreciating chips on a mountain of borrowed money. Long-term debt has climbed to somewhere above **$17 billion**, up from around $14 billion only months earlier, with billions more in current obligations stacked on top — a total debt load pushing toward the mid-$20-billions, against an equity base so thin the debt-to-equity ratio runs into the double digits. And the company is not slowing down. Its capital-expenditure guidance for the year runs to **$30–35 billion** — more, in a single year, than it will book in revenue — as it races to buy the next generation of chips before the last one finishes melting.

This is the machine in full: borrow tens of billions, spend it on hardware that begins depreciating the moment it is racked, rent that hardware out under multi-year contracts, and use the contracted revenue to service the debt and borrow still more to buy the next batch. It works beautifully as long as three things hold at once — that demand keeps rising, that the chips hold their value longer than the skeptics fear, and that the credit markets keep lending against an asset base that is, by its nature, evaporating. Pull any one of the three and the structure does not bend. It snaps.

## The circle, again

There is a pattern that recurs in the most richly valued corners of the AI boom, and CoreWeave is one of its purest expressions: a small number of giant players passing enormous sums among themselves, each transaction validating the next.

Consider Nvidia. It is CoreWeave's **supplier** — the source of the GPUs that are the company's entire reason to exist, and the gatekeeper of who gets them first during shortages. It is also CoreWeave's **investor**, holding a stake worth well over a billion dollars. And it is, remarkably, CoreWeave's **backstop customer**: under an arrangement reported to run through 2032 and worth as much as **$6.3 billion**, Nvidia has agreed to buy up CoreWeave's *unsold* compute capacity — to be the buyer of last resort for the very chips it sold CoreWeave in the first place. Nvidia sells the shovels, funds the digger, and promises to buy back any gold he fails to find. That is not a market. It is a closed loop wearing the costume of one.

The customer concentration on the other side of the ledger is just as vivid. Microsoft has been the anchor since before the IPO, accounting for the majority of revenue — a figure reported around **62% in 2024 and roughly two-thirds in 2025.** A single customer providing two-thirds of your revenue is not a moat; it is a hostage situation, and it runs both ways. CoreWeave has worked to diversify — a reported **$14.2 billion** deal with Meta running through 2031, vast contracts with OpenAI — and now says no single customer will exceed about 35% of contracted revenue. But the new customers are the same handful of AI labs burning the same venture and corporate billions, and they are, increasingly, the same companies building their own data centers. The diversification is real. So is the circularity it is diversifying into.

## From crypto to colossus

It is worth remembering what this company was. CoreWeave began life as a crypto-mining operation, a warehouse of GPUs pointed at Ethereum, until the founders had the timing and the nerve to repoint the same basic bet at artificial intelligence. That pivot was genuinely visionary, and it made them billionaires. It is also a useful piece of context for reading the present moment, because it tells you the founders have done this before: they have watched a GPU-mining boom inflate on the promise that the machines would print money forever, and they have seen what happened to the people still holding the hardware when the economics turned. They know what the end of a compute gold rush looks like. They are, right now, selling.

The market, for its part, has begun to flinch. After roughly tripling from its IPO price, CoreWeave's stock has rolled over — down about a quarter from its spring high near **$138**, changing hands closer to **$102**, with short interest above 10% and the chartists pointing at bearish patterns. The valuation still embeds heroic growth: a price-to-sales multiple north of eight on a company that loses money, carries double-digit leverage, and depends on the kindness of three customers and one supplier. Revenue is, to be fair, exploding — more than doubling year over year, with a contracted backlog reported near $100 billion. The bull case is not stupid. It is simply a bet that the music keeps playing long enough for the contracts to outrun the depreciation and the debt.

## The quietest sell signal

None of this is proof of wrongdoing. The 10b5-1 plans are legal. The useful-life assumption is within the range auditors have blessed. The debt is disclosed, the customer concentration is in the filings, the Nvidia arrangements are public. Everything here is hiding in plain sight, which is exactly what makes it a story worth telling rather than a scandal waiting to break.

But step back and look at what the insiders are doing with the one resource that cannot lie: their own money. The founders who understand this business better than any analyst, who can see the real utilization curves and the real replacement schedules and the real conversations with Microsoft, have spent the months since their lockup expired converting $2.3 billion of conviction into cash — and have not, between them, bought back a single share at any price. The hedge fund that backed them earliest has taken $5.5 billion off the table. The only large holder standing pat is the one whose true profit comes from selling CoreWeave the chips, not from owning the stock.

When the people who built the machine are quietly stepping off it, and the only ones still climbing aboard are the ones who cannot see the depreciation schedule, that is not a growth story. It is a tell. And the house, as always, is reading it long before the table does.
