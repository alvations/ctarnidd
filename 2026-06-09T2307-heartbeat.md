# Heartbeat

*Several times a year, on the trading charts of America's biggest funds, a strange spike appears and vanishes — a sudden surge of money in, then symmetrically out, like the blip on a heart monitor. It is not a glitch. It is the sound of capital-gains taxes being legally erased, and it costs the US Treasury an estimated $48 billion a year.*

Pull up the daily fund-flow chart of a large American exchange-traded fund and watch it for a year, and most of the time it is unremarkable — a gentle drift of money in and out as investors buy and sell. But every so often, often near the end of a quarter, the line does something violent and symmetrical: billions of dollars surge *into* the fund in a single day, and then, a day or two later, almost exactly the same amount surges *out.* On a chart it looks like the spike on a hospital heart monitor — a flat line, a sudden sharp peak, and back to flat. The people who run these funds call it, without irony, a **heartbeat.**

It is one of the most important financial maneuvers most investors have never heard of, and it is happening, quietly and legally, at enormous scale, right now, on the screens in front of anyone who knows where to look. A heartbeat trade is the mechanism by which the modern ETF industry makes capital-gains taxes disappear. According to Bloomberg's analysis, the tax break it enables costs the United States Treasury on the order of **$48 billion every year** — a subsidy, invisible on any budget, paid by every taxpayer to the holders of appreciated stock, pulsing across the market in spikes that look like a vital sign because, for the business model of the fund industry, that is exactly what they are.

## The trick, in plain sight

The mechanism is elegant, which is part of why it has gone unexamined for so long. To understand it you have to understand one quirk of how ETFs are built.

When a mutual fund needs to sell a stock that has risen in value — because an index dropped it, say, or the manager wants out — it sells the shares for cash, realizes a capital gain, and is required to pass that gain on to its investors, who owe tax on it whether or not they sold anything. This is why mutual-fund holders sometimes receive an unwelcome tax bill in a year their fund went *down*. ETFs are different. They can hand securities *out* the door "in kind," to a large trading firm called an authorized participant, rather than selling them — and under **Section 852(b)(6)** of the US tax code, when a fund disposes of an appreciated security through such an in-kind redemption, **no capital gain is recognized at all.** The gain does not get deferred to a later sale by the fund. From the fund's perspective, it simply evaporates.

The heartbeat is how a fund uses this on demand. When an appreciated stock needs to leave — typically around an index rebalancing — the manager asks a friendly bank or market maker to make a huge, temporary investment: the firm creates billions of dollars of new ETF shares for a day or two (the inflow spike). Then it redeems those shares, and the fund hands back, *in kind*, exactly the low-basis, highly-appreciated stock it wanted to shed (the outflow spike). The bank gets its money back, plus a fee for its trouble; the fund has flushed its embedded gains out the door without ever triggering a taxable sale; and the investors who remain owe nothing. The whole round trip is the heartbeat. It does nothing for the fund's actual investment portfolio — it is purely a tax maneuver — and that is precisely why it shows up on the flow chart as a spike that arrives and departs leaving the holdings unchanged.

## The 2019 door

For most of ETF history, this technique was a privilege of the few. Using it required special, individual permission from the Securities and Exchange Commission, and only a handful of giant managers — Vanguard, BlackRock — had obtained it. Then, in 2019, the SEC adopted a rule, known as the ETF Rule, that simplified the "custom basket" process and effectively opened the door to *every* ETF. The maneuver that had been a quiet advantage of the largest index providers became a tool available to the entire industry, and the industry took it.

The scale that followed is the part that should arrest you. By recent estimates, funds shed roughly **$293 billion of assets through heartbeat trades in a single year** — a third of a trillion dollars of appreciated securities passed out the back door, tax-free, in EKG-shaped pulses. And the maneuver has become a structural engine of the market's evolution: it is one of the central reasons money has poured out of mutual funds and into ETFs, and a particular reason for the explosive growth of *actively managed* ETFs, which lean on heartbeats far more heavily than plain index funds — heartbeats made up an estimated **18% of net daily outflows** in active ETFs in a recent year, against **9%** for passive ones. The tax tail is wagging the entire dog of how Americans now invest.

## Defer, or dodge?

The industry's defenders make a narrow, technically-true point: heartbeat trades, they say, *defer* taxes rather than eliminate them. The investor who eventually sells the ETF will pay capital-gains tax on the whole appreciation then; all the heartbeat does is postpone the annual drip of distributions until that final sale. By this telling, the $48 billion is a timing difference, not a giveaway.

It is not that simple, and the gap is where the real money lives. Deferral itself has enormous value — an unpaid tax that compounds in your account for decades is close to an interest-free loan from the government. But more than that, for a great many holders the deferral becomes outright *avoidance*, because of another feature of the tax code: when an investor dies, their heirs inherit the shares with a "stepped-up" cost basis, and the lifetime of accumulated, never-taxed gains is wiped clean — forever. The heartbeat defers the gain to the moment of sale; the step-up at death cancels it entirely. For the wealthy holders who can afford to buy and never sell, the combination is not a postponement. It is a permanent, legal erasure of capital-gains tax on a third of a trillion dollars a year of appreciation, and the $48 billion the Treasury forgoes does not come back. Academics at Chicago and Fordham have called for "unplugging" heartbeat trades; Brookings has mapped the reform options. Nothing has happened, because the constituency that benefits is large, wealthy, and organized, and the constituency that pays — every other taxpayer — does not know the spike on the chart has a name.

## The vital sign

There is something quietly remarkable about the fact that one of the largest tax subsidies in American finance is *visible*, in real time, to anyone with a fund-flow chart, and yet almost invisible in the political and public conversation. The heartbeat does not hide. It pulses in plain sight, several times a year, on the most widely-held funds in the country, a recurring spike that any data terminal will draw for you. It is hidden not by secrecy but by complexity and by boredom — by the reasonable assumption of ordinary investors that the mechanical plumbing of fund administration could not possibly be where forty-eight billion dollars a year quietly changes hands.

None of this is fraud, and none of it is even, strictly, a loophole in the pejorative sense: it is the considered consequence of a tax provision Congress wrote and a rule the SEC deliberately broadened. The ETF structure delivers genuine benefits — it has lifted long-term investors' after-tax returns by roughly a percentage point a year over comparable mutual funds, which over a lifetime is a great deal of real money kept by real savers. The tax efficiency is a feature, and millions of middle-class retirement accounts benefit from it too.

But it is worth seeing the heartbeat for what it is, because it is a near-perfect emblem of how modern markets actually work: an enormous, consequential, legal machine, operating in full view and near-total obscurity at the same time, redistributing tens of billions of dollars a year through a maneuver so technical that the people paying for it will never notice the charge. The next time someone tells you the market is a level playing field, pull up the flow chart of a big ETF near quarter-end and wait for the spike. That sudden surge in and symmetric surge out — the flat line, the peak, the flat line again — is the sound of the system breathing. It has a pulse. It has always had a pulse. Most investors have simply never been shown the monitor.
