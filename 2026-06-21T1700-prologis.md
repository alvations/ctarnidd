# Prologis trades at 21x FFO while its rent-rollover engine has lost two-thirds of its thrust

*Prologis raised guidance, posted record leasing, and called a market inflection — and the stock, near $128, prices that story as if the supercycle never left. But strip the narrative and look at the one number that built this company's legend: the re-leasing spread. Net effective rent change on rollover ran 84% at the 3Q23 peak; it was 31.9% in the first quarter of 2026. Cash rent change has fallen from 54% to 16.8% over the same span. The embedded mark-to-market gain — the discount between in-place rents and market rents that Prologis can harvest as leases expire — has shrunk from roughly 60%-plus at the top to about 17% today. That gap is the company's organic growth engine, and it is emptying. The market is paying a premium AFFO multiple on a same-store number that is now leaning on falling bad debt and a development pivot into data centers — not on the warehouse re-rating that minted the last decade's returns.*

There is a particular kind of company that is most dangerous to short precisely when it is doing well. Prologis, the world's largest owner of warehouses, is having a good year by its own scorecard. On April 16, 2026, it reported first-quarter Core FFO of $1.50 per share — $1.52 excluding net promote expense — up from $1.42 a year earlier. It signed a record 64 million square feet of leases. It pushed period-end occupancy to 95.3%. It raised full-year Core FFO guidance to $6.07–$6.23, lifted occupancy and cash same-store NOI ranges, and told investors that U.S. market rents had ticked up for the first time in two and a half years. Management framed the quarter as the moment the cycle turned back in its favor.

The stock, near $128 and carrying a market capitalization around $132 billion, reflects that confidence. At the midpoint of guidance, roughly $6.15 of Core FFO, the shares trade near 21 times FFO — a premium multiple for a real estate company, the kind of multiple equities award to compounders with long runways of organic growth. That is the heart of the matter. Prologis is not priced as a cyclical landlord. It is priced as a structural beneficiary of e-commerce, a secular grower whose rents only ever ratchet up. The forensic question is whether the engine that earned that premium is still running at the speed the multiple implies. The evidence in Prologis's own disclosures says it has slowed dramatically — and that the headline numbers cheering investors are being held up by accounting tailwinds and a strategic pivot away from the very business the story is about.

## The re-leasing spread, the one number that built the legend, has collapsed

Industrial REITs do not grow the way technology companies grow. They grow by signing leases at higher rents than the expiring leases they replace. For most of the last decade, Prologis did this with extraordinary force. When a warehouse lease signed in, say, 2018 expired in 2023, the market rent for that same box had often doubled. Prologis would re-lease it and book a spread — what it calls "net effective rent change" — that ran into the high double digits. This is the company's organic growth flywheel, and it was spinning at a once-in-a-generation rate.

Consider the trajectory, drawn from Prologis's own quarterly supplements. In the third quarter of 2023, net effective rent change on rollover hit an all-time high of 84.0%, with cash rent change of 54.2%. By the first quarter of 2025 those figures had fallen to 53.7% and 32.1%. In the third quarter of 2025 they were 49.4% and 29.4%. And in the first quarter of 2026 — the quarter Wall Street cheered — net effective rent change was 31.9% and cash rent change was just 16.8%.

Read those numbers slowly, because they are the whole thesis. The spread that defines Prologis's earning power has fallen by roughly two-thirds from its peak in under three years. The cash version — the one that actually shows up in collected rent — has fallen by nearly 70%. A 16.8% cash spread is still positive; warehouses are not being re-leased at a loss. But it is a fraction of the 50%-plus engine that justified the secular story. The flywheel is still turning. It is turning at idle.

## The denominator that should worry you: embedded mark-to-market is nearly spent

The bull case has long pointed to Prologis's "lease mark-to-market" — the gap between the below-market rents on its in-place leases and what those spaces would fetch today. As long as that gap exists, the company has guaranteed organic upside baked in: every expiring lease re-prices upward, and the spread cushions same-store growth for years to come. This was the great reassurance. The cycle could pause, demand could wobble, and Prologis would still mechanically grind rents higher as old leases rolled.

But a stock of embedded upside is a depleting reserve, not a renewable resource. At the end of 2025, Prologis estimated its net-effective lease mark-to-market at roughly 18%, which it framed as around $800 million of future NOI to be captured over the coming years. In the first quarter of 2026, that figure was about 17%. Think about what that means structurally. The company is harvesting the gap by re-leasing at higher rents — which closes the gap — faster than market rent growth refills it. A mark-to-market that has fallen from the 60%-plus implied at the top of the cycle to 17% today is a tank that is most of the way to empty. When that number approaches the low single digits, the mechanical tailwind that has powered same-store NOI for a decade simply stops, and growth becomes a pure bet on new market-rent inflation — exactly the cyclical, supply-sensitive variable the secular framing was supposed to let investors ignore.

## What is actually holding same-store NOI up: falling bad debt, not pricing power

Here is where quality-of-earnings discipline matters. Prologis reported cash same-store NOI growth of 8.8% in the first quarter — a robust-looking figure that supports the raised guidance and the premium multiple. But management itself attributed the strength to three things: higher occupancy, rent change, and "unusually low bad debt." That third driver deserves scrutiny, because it is not pricing power. It is the absence of tenant defaults.

Bad debt is a swing factor that flatters a quarter when it is low and savages one when it normalizes. A landlord that collects nearly all of its billed rent in a benign credit environment posts a same-store number boosted by the simple fact that fewer tenants stiffed it than usual. That tailwind is, by definition, not repeatable; bad debt cannot fall below zero, and it tends to revert upward when the economy softens — precisely the moment a warehouse owner is most exposed. The net-effective same-store figure, which Prologis reported at around 6.1%, sits well below the cash 8.8% headline, and the gap is a tell: the cash number is being lifted by timing and collection benefits that the smoother accrual measure strips out. An investor paying 21 times FFO for "durable" same-store growth is, in part, paying for a quarter in which not enough tenants went bankrupt.

## Cyclical priced as secular: vacancy off the floor, supply still landing

The secular framing rests on a tidy story: e-commerce permanently expands warehouse demand, so industrial real estate is a one-way ratchet. Reality is more cyclical, and Prologis's own market data shows it. U.S. warehouse vacancy, which sat near record lows in 2022, has risen meaningfully and was running around 7.5% in early 2026 — flat sequentially, but well off the floor that powered the rent explosion. Coastal markets, the crown jewels, saw rents fall year over year in the recent cycle before the recent stabilization. This is what the top of a real estate cycle looks like in slow motion: vacancy normalizes upward, new supply that was greenlit during the boom continues to deliver, and the pricing power that comes from genuine scarcity fades.

Prologis is now telling investors the worst is behind, that supply is rolling over and rents are inflecting back up. They may be right — more on that below. But notice the logical pivot. For years the pitch was that demand was secular, so you did not have to time the cycle. Now the pitch is that the cycle has bottomed and is turning, which is an explicitly cyclical, supply-and-demand argument. A company that needs you to believe in a freshly-timed cyclical inflection to justify a premium multiple is, definitionally, not the secular compounder the multiple assumes. You cannot have it both ways: either rents only go up (false, as 2024's coastal declines showed), or they are cyclical and you are being asked to underwrite a turn that has not yet proven itself in cash rents.

## The pivot tells the story: when warehouses stall, sell data centers

The most revealing line in the first-quarter release was not about warehouses at all. Of Prologis's $1.78 billion in development starts, $1.3 billion was data centers, and the company has loudly accelerated its data-center ambitions, framing them as the next leg of growth. There is a sound business logic to it — Prologis controls land and power-adjacent sites that are valuable for AI infrastructure. But step back and ask why a company whose entire equity story is "permanent e-commerce-driven warehouse demand" is redirecting the majority of its new capital into a completely different asset class.

The honest read is that the warehouse re-rating is largely played out, and management knows it needs a new growth narrative to feed a multiple built for double-digit organic compounding. Data centers are that narrative. But a data-center development business is not the high-moat, low-capital, mark-to-market machine that warehouses were at the top of the cycle. It is capital-intensive, competitive, exposed to a hyperscaler customer base that can build its own capacity, and dependent on power availability and a single secular bet — AI demand — that is itself richly priced across the market. Investors who pay 21 times FFO for warehouse durability are, increasingly, paying it for a speculative bet on AI infrastructure execution dressed in an industrial REIT's clothing. That is a different risk than the one the multiple was struck against.

## Customer concentration: the demand story has a few very large faces

The "secular e-commerce demand" thesis has a concentration problem that the aggregate vacancy statistics obscure. Prologis's demand is not a diffuse cloud of millions of small merchants; it is dominated by a handful of enormous logistics tenants, with Amazon historically its single largest customer. The health of Prologis's leasing pipeline is therefore tethered to the capital-spending appetites of a small number of giants — companies that have, in recent years, swung from aggressive warehouse expansion to digesting overbuilt footprints and subleasing excess space.

This matters for the secular framing because it means the demand curve is lumpy and policy-sensitive in a way "structural e-commerce growth" papers over. When the largest tenants pause expansion, slow their take of new space, or push existing leases out, the marginal pricing on rollover — the very spread that has collapsed from 84% to 32% — feels it immediately. A landlord whose growth depends on a concentrated set of mega-tenants re-accelerating their build-out is not insulated from the cycle; it is leveraged to the capital-allocation mood of a few boardrooms. The record 64 million square feet of first-quarter signings is genuinely impressive, but it is a volume statistic, not a price one — and volume at a 16.8% cash spread is a very different animal than volume at a 54% spread.

## Adjusted versus GAAP: the promote and the gap to net earnings

Prologis, like most large REITs, leads with Core FFO, an adjusted metric that excludes depreciation, certain gains, and "net promote expense." That is conventional and defensible — depreciation genuinely understates the economics of appreciating real estate. But the gap between the adjusted story and the GAAP reality is worth keeping in view. First-quarter Core FFO was $1.50 per share; GAAP net earnings were $1.05 per diluted share. The full-year guidance spreads even wider: Core FFO of $6.07–$6.23 against net earnings of just $3.80–$4.05.

Some of that gap is the legitimate depreciation add-back. But the practice of presenting two versions of FFO — one "including net promote expense" and one "excluding" it — is precisely the kind of adjusted-number layering that lets a company steer attention to the most flattering figure. Promote income, the performance fees Prologis earns managing third-party capital, is lumpy and market-dependent; carving its expense out of the headline smooths a number that is inherently bumpy. None of this is improper, and Prologis discloses it cleanly. The point for a buyer paying 21 times the adjusted figure is simply that the multiple is struck against the most generous available measure of earnings, on a quarter flattered by low bad debt, for a business whose core organic spread has fallen by two-thirds. The margin of safety in that arithmetic is thin.

## Priced for perfection: the asymmetry in the multiple

Put the valuation and the operating trend side by side and the asymmetry becomes the whole argument. At roughly 21 times Core FFO with a forward dividend yield around 3.0–3.2% and a dividend that already consumes about three-quarters of FFO, Prologis is priced for continued mid-to-high-single-digit FFO growth indefinitely. To deliver that, it needs the re-leasing spread to stabilize and re-accelerate, the embedded mark-to-market to refill via fresh market-rent inflation, occupancy to hold above 95%, bad debt to stay benign, and the data-center pivot to compound — all at once.

Now consider the downside path, which requires nothing exotic. If the cyclical "inflection" management is calling proves premature and market rents stay soft, the mark-to-market keeps depleting toward the low single digits, the cash spread on rollover keeps grinding down from 16.8%, bad debt normalizes off its unusually low base, and same-store NOI growth decelerates from the 6–9% range toward the low single digits that a mature, fully-marked portfolio actually generates. In that world, the premium multiple compresses toward something more like a normal industrial REIT — and at 21 times FFO, multiple compression does far more damage than the dividend yield repays. That is the textbook priced-for-perfection setup: limited upside if everything goes right, meaningful downside if the cyclical call is early. The market is offering you the wrong side of that trade.

## What the bulls genuinely get right

It would be dishonest to pretend this is a broken business, because it is anything but. Prologis is, on almost every operational measure, best-in-class, and the bull case has real substance that a fair forensic account must concede.

First, the assets are irreplaceable. Prologis owns infill logistics real estate in supply-constrained markets — locations near major population centers where new warehouse supply is genuinely hard to permit and build. That scarcity is structural, not cyclical, and it underpins long-run pricing power that a low-quality portfolio simply cannot replicate. Second, the balance sheet is a fortress: debt-to-adjusted-EBITDA around 4.8x and a low cost of capital give Prologis the ability to develop, acquire, and weather downturns from a position of strength that smaller peers lack. Third, the operating metrics are genuinely strong right now — 95.3% occupancy, 75.8% retention, record leasing volume, and a still-positive double-digit cash spread are not the signatures of a company in trouble. A 16.8% cash re-leasing spread, even after the collapse from the peak, is a level most landlords in most asset classes would envy.

Fourth, and most importantly, the cyclical-inflection call might be right. Management's read — that new supply is rolling over and U.S. market rents have begun to tick up after a long pause — is plausible, and Prologis has better real-time visibility into warehouse demand than almost anyone. If rents re-accelerate, the mark-to-market refills, the spread stabilizes higher, and the data-center optionality compounds, then 21 times FFO will look reasonable in hindsight and the bears will have mistaken a mid-cycle pause for a top. The data-center pivot, too, is built on a real asset advantage in land and power. The dividend is well-covered and growing. None of these strengths are illusory. The short case is not that Prologis is bad; it is that Prologis is priced as if the best is permanent, when its own disclosures show the best has already decelerated by two-thirds and now leans on a freshly-timed cyclical bet.

## The kicker

Strip away the record-leasing headline and the data-center excitement and you are left with a single forensic fact that the multiple refuses to acknowledge: the spread that built Prologis has fallen from 84% to 32%, the cash version from 54% to 17%, and the embedded upside in its leases is most of the way spent. The company raised guidance on a quarter flattered by occupancy gains and unusually low bad debt, then redirected the bulk of its new capital into a different asset class entirely — the surest sign that the warehouse re-rating is largely over. At 21 times FFO, you are paying a compounder's price for a landlord whose organic engine has downshifted into the cyclical gear it was supposed to have escaped, on the promise of a rent inflection that has not yet shown up in cash rents.

*The bet embedded in Prologis at $128 is that the rent supercycle merely paused; its own quarterly supplements suggest it instead ran most of the way out, and the market is being asked to pay top-of-cycle prices for the empty part of the tank.*
