# Cloudflare Is Priced for Perfect Software Margins Just as Its Own Start to Slip

*Cloudflare is one of the most admired infrastructure companies on the internet — a developer favorite with a
global network, a beloved platform, and the kind of 30%-plus growth that makes investors forgive almost any price.
In its first quarter of 2026 it grew revenue 34% to $639.8 million, expanded its roster of six- and seven-figure
customers dramatically, generated real free cash flow, and announced a bold pivot toward an agentic-AI operating
model. The market values it accordingly — among the very richest sales multiples in all of software, a valuation
that only makes sense if Cloudflare has the pristine, expanding gross margins that define the best software
businesses and will keep them for years. But buried in the same results is a number moving the wrong way: gross
margin fell to 71.2% from nearly 75% a year earlier. For a software company supposedly enjoying ever-improving
economies of scale, a gross margin that declines as the business grows is not a footnote — it is a tell, a hint
that the next leg of Cloudflare's growth carries structurally worse unit economics than the last. This is the
story of a company priced for perfect software margins at the precise moment its own have begun to slip.*

Begin with the genuine quality, because Cloudflare has earned its reputation. In the first quarter of 2026 it grew
revenue 34% year over year to $639.8 million, guided to roughly 30% growth for the full year toward $2.81 billion,
and posted standout enterprise momentum: 4,416 customers now spend more than $100,000 a year with it, and deals
exceeding $1 million grew 73% year over year. It generated $84.1 million of free cash flow, about 13% of revenue,
and reported non-GAAP net income of $94 million. Its global network, its developer platform, and its security
products are genuinely loved by the engineers who use them, and that affection is a real and underappreciated
moat. This is, by almost any operating measure, an excellent company.

But an excellent company and a sound investment are not the same thing, and the difference is almost entirely a
matter of price. Cloudflare trades at one of the highest revenue multiples in software — a valuation that prices
not just continued rapid growth but the durable, high, expanding gross margins that justify paying for software
rather than for infrastructure. So this essay examines the one number quietly contradicting that premise, the gap
between the company's non-GAAP and GAAP profitability, the capital-intensive AI ambition that may be compressing
the margins, and the dramatic restructuring that the company has dressed as confidence.

## The gross margin is the tell

Start with the number that should trouble a buyer paying a software multiple: gross margin fell to 71.2%, down from
nearly 75% a year earlier. In most businesses a few points of gross margin would be noise. In a company valued at a
premium-software multiple, it is the single most important diagnostic, because the entire case for paying twenty or
thirty times revenue rather than the low single digits a hardware or infrastructure company commands rests on the
belief that software gross margins are high, stable, and tend to expand as a business scales — that each
incremental dollar of revenue costs almost nothing to deliver, so growth flows to profit. A gross margin that
declines as revenue grows is the opposite signal: it says the incremental dollar is costing more to deliver, not
less, and that the company is scaling into territory with worse unit economics than the business it came from.

The likely explanation makes the signal more concerning, not less. Cloudflare's newer growth vectors — running AI
inference at the edge, hosting compute-intensive workloads, building out the network and hardware to support them
— are structurally more capital- and cost-intensive than its legacy content-delivery and security services. Those
older products were close to pure software, delivered over a network already built; the newer AI and compute
products require expensive silicon, power, and capacity, and they carry the lower gross margins of a business that
actually has to run heavy workloads rather than route packets. If the future of Cloudflare's growth is
increasingly AI inference and compute, then the future of Cloudflare's gross margin is structurally lower than its
past — and the premium-software multiple, which assumes the margin holds or expands, is pricing exactly the wrong
trajectory. The falling gross margin is not a one-quarter blip to be explained away; it may be the leading edge of
a mix shift that erodes the very economics the valuation depends on.

## The profit you see is the adjusted one

There is the familiar matter of which profit figure the company leads with. Cloudflare reports $94 million of
non-GAAP net income, a healthy-looking number — but non-GAAP profitability for a high-growth software company
typically excludes a large amount of stock-based compensation, and the GAAP profitability that remains after that
real cost is counted is materially thinner, and for much of Cloudflare's history has hovered near breakeven or
loss. This is the same adjusted-versus-real gap that recurs across richly valued growth companies: the cash
generation is real, the non-GAAP profit is real as far as it goes, but the headline that makes the company look
solidly profitable is the one that sets aside the cost of paying employees in stock — a cost paid not in cash but
in the steady dilution of existing shareholders.

For a company at Cloudflare's valuation, the distinction matters because the multiple is already pricing years of
margin expansion and eventual GAAP profitability as a near-certainty. If gross margins are slipping at the same
time that the path to clean GAAP profit runs through still-heavy stock compensation, then the two pillars of the
profitability story — high gross margin converting to operating leverage, and operating leverage converting to
real earnings — are both shakier than the non-GAAP headline suggests. The bull sees a fast-growing, free-cash-flow-
generating platform; the skeptic sees a company whose reported profitability leans on adjustments precisely as the
underlying margin that should power real profitability turns down.

It is worth adding that free cash flow itself, while genuine and a real mark of discipline, deserves a careful eye
in a capital-intensive infrastructure business. Cloudflare's $84 million of free cash flow is reported after its
capital spending, which is to its credit — but as the company leans further into AI inference and compute, the
capital intensity of the business tends to rise, because running heavy workloads requires buying and powering
expensive hardware. A free-cash-flow margin that looks healthy today can be squeezed tomorrow if the next phase of
growth demands a step-up in capital expenditure on data centers and chips, the same spending that is already
visible in the falling gross margin. The cash generation and the gross margin are not separate stories; they are
two readings of the same underlying shift toward a heavier, more capital-hungry business, and an investor paying
for software-like capital efficiency should watch both for signs that the AI ambition is quietly making Cloudflare
more like an infrastructure operator than the asset-light platform its multiple assumes.

## The AI pivot that fights the hyperscalers on their turf

The strategic heart of Cloudflare's growth story is artificial intelligence — specifically, the ambition to run AI
inference at the edge of its network, close to users, as the place where a great deal of the world's AI computation
will eventually happen. It is a genuinely exciting vision, and Cloudflare's distributed network is a real asset for
it. But it is also a vision that walks Cloudflare directly into competition with the largest, best-capitalized
companies on earth — Amazon, Microsoft, and Google, the hyperscale cloud providers who dominate AI compute, own
their own silicon, and can outspend Cloudflare on data centers and chips by orders of magnitude.

This is the strategic risk the multiple does not appear to price. Cloudflare's historical success came in domains
where it could out-innovate and undercut incumbents with a smarter, lighter, software-defined network — CDN,
security, edge functions. AI inference compute is a different kind of battlefield: it is capital-intensive, it
rewards scale of physical infrastructure, and the competitors are the deepest-pocketed firms in the world, the same
firms whose capital expenditure on AI now runs into the tens of billions per quarter each. Cloudflare can carve out
a real and valuable niche at the edge, and its network gives it genuine advantages for latency-sensitive inference.
But the dream of capturing a vast slice of AI compute is a dream of competing where the hyperscalers are strongest
and where the economics are heaviest — and the falling gross margin is the early evidence that this fight costs
more, per dollar of revenue, than the software business it is replacing. An infinite-seeming AI TAM is the bull's
favorite number; it is also, here, the margin's adversary.

## The restructuring dressed as confidence

Then there is the 20% workforce reduction — roughly 1,100 employees — announced as a decisive pivot to an
"agentic AI-driven operating model." The framing is confident, even visionary: Cloudflare is reorganizing itself
around AI, using its own technology to run leaner, and positioning for the future. And it may genuinely be that;
the cut could make the company structurally more profitable and more focused, and management has the credibility
to attempt it.

But a 20% headcount reduction is also a large and abrupt action, and abrupt large actions deserve to be read in
more than one way. Cutting a fifth of the workforce is what a company does when it concludes its cost structure was
not scaling the way it needed to — when the path to the profitability the valuation assumes required a step-change
in costs rather than the smooth operating leverage a great software business is supposed to produce naturally. A
company whose economics were compounding cleanly does not usually need to remove a fifth of its people to get
there. So the layoff can be read as the confident pivot it is presented as, or as an admission that the margin
expansion the market is paying for was not arriving on its own and had to be manufactured through cuts. Both
readings can be partly true at once, and an investor paying a premium-software multiple should at least weigh the
less flattering one, because a business that has to shrink its way toward the profitability its price assumes is a
different proposition than one growing into it effortlessly.

## What a premium-sales multiple actually demands

It is worth being explicit about the arithmetic of a valuation set at twenty or thirty times revenue, because the
number sounds abstract until you translate it into the performance it requires. A company trading at such a
multiple is being priced as though it will not merely keep growing quickly but will eventually convert a large
fraction of a much bigger future revenue base into high-margin profit — the multiple effectively borrows years of
flawless future results and books them into today's price. For that to pay off, several things must all go right
together: growth has to stay rapid for a long time, gross margins have to hold or expand so that growth becomes
profit, operating costs have to fall as a share of revenue, and competition has to fail to compress any of it.

The danger in such a structure is its asymmetry. If every one of those conditions holds, the stock merely earns
into a price that already assumed they would; the upside from here is the reward for being right about perfection.
But if even one condition slips — growth decelerates a little, or gross margin keeps sliding, or the AI compute
business proves lower-margin than hoped, or a hyperscaler prices aggressively against Cloudflare's edge offering —
the multiple itself can compress, and multiple compression on a high-multiple stock is brutal, because the price
falls both on the lower earnings expectation and on the lower multiple applied to it, a double hit. At a premium
valuation, in other words, being right gets you a fair return and being slightly wrong gets you a large loss, and
the falling gross margin is the first concrete sign that one of the load-bearing assumptions may not hold. The
valuation has left no room for the ordinary imperfection that almost every company eventually exhibits.

## The cautionary tale next door

Cloudflare's investors do not have to imagine what happens when a beloved edge-infrastructure company priced for
perfection stumbles, because there is a recent example in its own neighborhood. Fastly, a direct competitor in
content delivery and edge computing, was once a market darling trading at a rich multiple on a similar promise of
edge-computing dominance — and when its growth decelerated and a large customer concentration came to light, the
stock collapsed by the large majority of its value and never recovered its former standing. The business did not
vanish; it simply turned out to be worth a fraction of what the priced-for-perfection valuation had assumed.

The point is not that Cloudflare is Fastly — Cloudflare is a far larger, more diversified, more impressively run
company with a much broader product portfolio and far deeper enterprise traction, and the comparison flatters
Fastly more than it indicts Cloudflare. The point is narrower and harder to dismiss: the edge-infrastructure
category has already demonstrated, once, how violently the market re-rates one of these companies when the
flawless-growth narrative cracks, and how little of the prior valuation survives the re-rating. Cloudflare's
quality buys it a wider margin of safety than Fastly ever had, but it does not exempt it from the same mechanics,
and the same mechanics are precisely what a premium multiple exposes an investor to. A great company at a perfect
price is still, in the end, dependent on the price being deserved — and the category's own history shows what the
fall looks like when it is not.

## What the bulls genuinely get right

In fairness, the bull case on Cloudflare is strong, and the company's quality is not in question — the debate is
the price. Cloudflare is genuinely beloved by developers, and that affection translates into a real bottom-up
adoption moat that is hard for competitors to replicate: engineers choose it, bring it into their companies, and
expand its footprint from the inside. Its growth is real and durable, sustained above 30% at meaningful scale,
with the 73% growth in million-dollar deals signaling that the enterprise traction the bears long doubted is
genuinely arriving. It generates real free cash flow, a discipline many high-growth peers lack. Its global network
is a genuine, capital-intensive asset that took years to build and cannot be easily copied, and its position in
edge computing and AI inference, if even partly realized, addresses an enormous and growing market. And the AI
pivot and restructuring, if executed well, could make the company both faster-growing and more profitable than it
has ever been. For investors who believe Cloudflare will become a defining infrastructure layer of the AI era, the
current margin wobble is a temporary cost of building toward something vast.

The honest synthesis is that Cloudflare is an exceptional, beloved, fast-growing infrastructure company whose
valuation prices that excellence as a near-certainty extending years into the future — and whose gross margin,
the single metric most central to that valuation, has begun to move the wrong way. The bull is right that the
platform, the developer love, the enterprise momentum, and the AI ambition are all real and formidable. The
skeptic notes that a premium-software multiple on a business with falling gross margins, adjusted-only
profitability, a capital-intensive AI pivot against the hyperscalers, and a 20% layoff to force the economics is a
price that has assumed perfection precisely as the first imperfections appear.

## The kicker

Cloudflare deserves its reputation: it is one of the genuinely special companies in internet infrastructure,
loved by the people who build on it, growing fast, generating cash, and reaching boldly for a role in the AI era.
None of that is in doubt. What is in doubt is whether a valuation among the richest in all of software can be
sustained by a business whose gross margin is slipping, whose headline profit is the adjusted kind, whose biggest
growth bet leads it onto the capital-heavy turf of the hyperscalers, and which just cut a fifth of its staff to
make the numbers work. A premium-software multiple is a bet on perfect software economics holding for years.
Cloudflare's economics are still very good — but they are, for the first time, visibly imperfect, and at this price
the distance between very good and perfect is the whole of the risk.

*The market is paying for software economics that compound forever — pristine margins widening as the network does the work for free — and Cloudflare has been, for most of its life, a fair embodiment of that dream; but the newest dollars of its growth are made of AI inference and compute, which cost real silicon and real power, and so the gross margin has begun, quietly, to bend downward, and a price built on the assumption that it never would now waits to learn whether the bend is a blip or the beginning of the business Cloudflare is becoming.*
